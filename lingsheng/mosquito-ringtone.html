Mosquito Ringtone: The Ultrasonic Sound for Teens

Is your phone ringing? If you are over 25, you might not even know it. Explore the science of the Mosquito tone.

What is a Mosquito Ringtone?

The Mosquito Ringtone (also known as the Teen Buzz) is a high-frequency sound, typically around 17.4 kHz. It is named after the "Mosquito" ultrasonic deterrent used to prevent teenagers from loitering. Because human hearing degrades with age—a condition known as Presbycusis—most adults over the age of 25-30 lose the ability to hear these extremely high frequencies.

The Science of Presbycusis

As we age, the tiny hair cells in the cochlea that detect high-frequency vibrations are the first to be damaged or die. Unlike skin cells, these hair cells do not regrow. This is why a sound at 17.4 kHz is loud and clear to a 15-year-old, but completely silent to a 40-year-old.

Typical Hearing Thresholds by Age:

Age Range Typical Max Frequency
Under 18 17 kHz - 20 kHz
19 - 24 15 kHz - 17 kHz
25 - 35 12 kHz - 15 kHz
40+ Under 12 kHz

Why Use a Mosquito Ringtone?

Aside from being a cool science experiment, teens often use this as a "Stealth Ringtone" in environments where they don't want adults or teachers to notice their phone receiving notifications. However, keep in mind that some high-quality audio equipment or household pets may still react strongly to the sound!

Production Note

When producing high-frequency tones, it's vital to use a high sample rate (at least 44.1 kHz or 48 kHz) to avoid **aliasing**—a digital artifact where high frequencies "fold back" into lower, audible ranges. Our download is rendered at 48kHz for maximum purity.
