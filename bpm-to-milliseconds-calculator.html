How to Use the BPM to MS Tool

In digital music production, timing is everything. Whether you are setting a delay time, adjusting a reverb's pre-delay, or fine-tuning an LFO rate, matching the time to the BPM (Beats Per Minute) of your song creates a "tighter" and more professional mix.

This BPM to MS calculator is bidirectional. If you know your tempo, enter it in the BPM box to see the delay times. Conversely, if you are working with a sample and know the length of a single beat in milliseconds, enter it in the MS box to find your project's BPM.

The Math Behind Tempo and Time

The relationship between BPM and milliseconds is a simple inverse calculation based on the fact that there are 60,000 milliseconds in one minute.

Core Calculation Formula

Milliseconds (for 1/4 note) = 60,000 / BPM

Example: At 120 BPM, one quarter note is 60,000 / 120 = 500ms.

Common Production Scenarios

  • Dotted Notes: Often used for "Galoping" delays. Multiply your base MS by 1.5.
  • Triplet Notes: Used for swing or shuffles. Divide your base MS by 3 and then multiply by 2 (or multiply by 0.667).
  • Reverb Pre-Delay: Setting your pre-delay to a 1/64 or 1/128 note value helps keep the reverb tail in time with the song, preventing "muddy" transients.

Why Sync Your Reverb?

If your reverb tail lasts too long, it will bleed into the next chord. By setting your decay time to a musical division (like 1/2 note), the reverb "breathes" with the rhythm.

DAW Tip

Most modern DAWs (Ableton, FL Studio, Logic) have a "Sync" button on plugins. However, knowing the physical MS value is essential when using hardware gear or vintage-style analog delay units.
