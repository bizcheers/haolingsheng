The Physics of the Fretboard

Understanding guitar note frequency is a vital skill for both players and producers. In standard tuning (E A D G B E), each open string is tuned to a specific reference pitch. As you press down on a fret, you effectively shorten the vibrating length of the string, which increases the frequency of the vibration.

Because the guitar uses equal temperament, moving up one fret multiplies the current frequency by approximately 1.05946 (the 12th root of 2). By the time you reach the 12th fret, the frequency has doubled, resulting in an octave jump.

Pro Tips for Mixing Guitars

Why do you need to know these numbers? In modern music production, knowing your frequencies allows for surgical EQing:

  • The Low E Bloom: The low E string (6th string, open) vibrates at 82.41 Hz. If your guitar track is booming or clashing with the kick drum, try a high-pass filter just below this frequency.
  • The "Body" Region: Most acoustic guitars have their "meat" in the 200 Hz to 400 Hz range. Referring to our Piano Frequency Chart, you'll see this corresponds to the middle section of the fretboard.
  • High-End Sparkle: Frequencies above 3 kHz (found on the highest frets of the E1 string) are responsible for pick noise and clarity.

Standard Tuning Reference

For quick reference, here are the fundamental frequencies of the open strings in standard A440 tuning:

  • E2 (6th String): 82.41 Hz
  • A2 (5th String): 110.00 Hz
  • D3 (4th String): 146.83 Hz
  • G3 (3rd String): 196.00 Hz
  • B3 (2nd String): 246.94 Hz
  • E4 (1st String): 329.63 Hz

Why A=440?

This tool uses the international standard pitch of 440 Hz for the note A4. If you tune to a different reference, all frequencies will shift proportionally.

The 12th Fret Rule

The 12th fret is exactly half the length of the string. This creates a frequency exactly double the open string pitch!
