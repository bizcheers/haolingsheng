How Pitch Shifting Works

In digital audio, pitch shifting is the process of changing the perceived pitch of a sound without necessarily changing its duration (though in simple "resampling" or tape-style effects, they change together). This process relies on a core mathematical ratio based on the power of 2.

Because the musical octave is defined by doubling the frequency, we divide that "double" into 1,200 equal parts called cents. A single semitone is exactly 100 cents. Therefore, to shift a sound up by one semitone, we multiply its frequency by the 12th root of 2 (approx. 1.05946).

The Pitch Shift Formula

To calculate the new frequency (f_new) from a base frequency (f_base) and a shift in semitones (n), we use the following formula:

f_new = f_base √ó 2^(n / 12)

If you are working with cents (c), the formula becomes:

f_new = f_base √ó 2^(c / 1200)

Practical Production Applications

  • Layering Samples: If you have a kick drum sample at 50 Hz and a sub-bass at 55 Hz, they might clash. Using this calculator, you can determine that shifting the kick up by 1.65 semitones would bring it perfectly in tune with the bass.
  • Avoiding Phasing: When doubling vocals, a slight pitch shift of +/- 5 to 10 cents can create a natural chorus effect and prevent destructive phasing.
  • Sound Design: Extreme pitch shifts (e.g., +12 or -12 semitones) are used to create "glitch" textures or to fit cinematic sound effects into the key of a song.

What are Cents?

A "Cent" is 1/100th of a semitone. It is the unit used for fine-tuning. Most humans cannot distinguish a pitch difference smaller than 5-10 cents.

Artifacting Note

Extreme pitch shifting (more than 1 octave) often introduces "artifacts" or metallic sounds unless you are using a high-quality "Polyphonic" or "Elastique" algorithm.
