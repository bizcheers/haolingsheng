Chords 101: Triads and Seventh Chords Explained

The Magic of Harmony

If melody is the "story" of a song, harmony (chords) is the "world" that story lives in. Chords set the mood, provide the foundation, and make music feel full. But how do we actually build them? If you've already explored our guides on the major scale and intervals, you have everything you need to become a chord-building pro.

What is a Triad?

A "triad" is the simplest type of chord. As the name suggests, it consists of exactly three notes. These notes aren't picked at random; they are built by stacking "thirds" on top of each other. Every triad has a Root, a Third, and a Fifth.

The Four Main Triads

Depending on the types of thirds you stack, you get different "flavors" of triads. The two most common ones are Major and Minor.

  • Major Triad: Root + Major 3rd + Perfect 5th. (Sounds bright, happy, stable).
  • Minor Triad: Root + Minor 3rd + Perfect 5th. (Sounds dark, sad, mysterious).
  • Diminished Triad: Root + Minor 3rd + Diminished 5th. (Sounds tense, unstable).
  • Augmented Triad: Root + Major 3rd + Augmented 5th. (Sounds dreamy, suspended).

Try it yourself: To build a C Major Triad, take the 1st, 3rd, and 5th notes of the C Major scale: C - E - G.

Leveling Up: Seventh Chords

Triads are great, but sometimes they can sound a bit "basic." If you want to add sophistication, jazziness, or more emotional weight, you add another note on top: the seventh. This results in a four-note chord called a "Seventh Chord."

Common Seventh Chords

  • Major 7th (Maj7): Major Triad + Major 7th. (Sounds lush, dreamy, romantic). Imagine a "C Maj7": C-E-G-B.
  • Dominant 7th (7): Major Triad + Minor 7th. (Sounds bluesy, wants to resolve). Imagine a "C7": C-E-G-Bb. This is the cornerstone of blues and rock progressions.
  • Minor 7th (m7): Minor Triad + Minor 7th. (Sounds mellow, soulful, sophisticated). Imagine a "Cm7": C-Eb-G-Bb.
  • Minor Major 7th (m/Maj7): Minor Triad + Major 7th. (The "James Bond" chord - very mysterious!).

Why Use Seventh Chords?

Adding that fourth note creates more interaction between the frequencies. In a Major 7th chord, the tension between the Root and the Major 7th (a half-step away from the octave) creates a beautiful "shimmer" that you don't get with a simple triad.

In songwriting, switching from a basic C chord to a C Maj7 can instantly change the vibe from a simple nursery rhyme to a professional-sounding ballad.

How to Practice Chord Building

The best way to learn is by doing. Pick a note, find its major scale, and try building the triad and the various seventh chords for that note.

  1. Start with the "Big Three": C, G, and F major.
  2. Master the fingering on your instrument for triads first.
  3. Experiment by adding the 7th note and noticing how the emotional color changes.
  4. Try playing through some common chord progressions using seventh chords instead of triads.

Final Thoughts

Don't be intimidated by the number of chord types out there. Even the most complex jazz chords are just extensions of these basic building blocks. If you understand triads and seventh chords, you have the foundation of 99% of western music.

Next time you're writing a song or learning a cover, look at the chords. Is it a simple triad? Or does it have that extra 7th note? Understanding why a chord sounds the way it does is the first step toward true musical freedom. Happy playing!

Chord Formulas

  • Major: 1 - 3 - 5
  • Minor: 1 - b3 - 5
  • Maj7: 1 - 3 - 5 - 7
  • m7: 1 - b3 - 5 - b7
  • Dom7: 1 - 3 - 5 - b7

Songwriting Tip

Try replacing the "I" chord in a ballad with a "Maj7" for an instant emotional boost.
