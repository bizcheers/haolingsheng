What is the Treble Clef?

The treble clef is the most common clef used in music. You'll see it on piano music, vocal sheet music, and music for instruments like the violin, flute, and trumpet. It is designed to represent higher-register pitches.

The "G Clef" Origin

The treble clef is also known as the G clef. If you look closely at the symbol, the large "curl" at the bottom of the clef wraps around the second line of the staff. In treble clef, that second line is always the note G.

Historically, the symbol itself evolved from an ornate letter 'G'. Knowing this "secret" helps you always find your bearings on the staff.

Reading the Spaces: FACE

One of the easiest ways to learn the notes of the treble clef is to memorize the spaces. From bottom to top, the four spaces represent the notes F, A, C, and E.

It's simple: The spaces spell FACE. This is one mnemonic you'll never forget!

Reading the Lines: EGBDF

The five lines (from bottom to top) represent E, G, B, D, and F. Since this doesn't spell a word, musicians use memory phrases:

  • Every Good Boy Does Fine
  • Every Good Boy Deserves Fruit
  • Elvis' Guitar Broke Down Friday
Choose the one that sticks in your mind!

Beyond the Staff: Ledger Lines

Sometimes music goes higher or lower than the five lines can show. In these cases, we use ledger lines. These are small horizontal lines added above or below the staff. The most famous ledger line note is Middle C, which sits on a single ledger line just below the treble clef staff.

Who Uses the Treble Clef?

Because it covers the range where the human voice typically speaks and sings (and where many instruments play), it's ubiquitous.

  • Woodwinds: Flute, Oboe, Clarinet, Saxophone
  • Strings: Violin
  • Brass: Trumpet, French Horn
  • Keyboard: Right hand of the Piano
