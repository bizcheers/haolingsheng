The Science of Digital Pitch

In digital music production, pitch is often represented by MIDI Note Numbers. The MIDI (Musical Instrument Digital Interface) standard defines a range of 128 notes, where MIDI 0 is a very low C (-1) and MIDI 127 is a very high G (9).

Unlike acoustic instruments which focus on physics and vibration, MIDI uses a linear integer system that is much easier for computers to process. However, for mixing, synthesis, and acoustic modeling, we must convert these integers back into physical frequencies (Hertz).

The Mathematical Formula

The conversion between a MIDI note number and its frequency follows a specific logarithmic formula based on the A4 = 440 Hz concert pitch standard.

Standard Formula (MIDI to Hz)

f = 2^((d - 69) / 12) √ó 440

Where d is the MIDI note number. The value 69 represents the MIDI number for A4, and 12 represents the number of semitones in an octave.

By using this formula, your DAW or synthesizer can determine the exact speed at which an oscillator should vibrate to produce the correct pitch. Every time you increase the MIDI number by 1, the frequency is multiplied by 1.05946 (the 12th root of 2).

Key Reference Points

While the calculator above handles any value, it's helpful for producers to memorize a few "anchor" points in the MIDI spectrum:

  • MIDI 21 (A0): 27.50 Hz - The lowest note on a standard piano.
  • MIDI 36 (C2): 65.41 Hz - A common root note for sub-bass in electronic music.
  • MIDI 60 (C4): 261.63 Hz - Middle C, the center of the musical keyboard.
  • MIDI 69 (A4): 440.00 Hz - The global tuning standard.
  • MIDI 108 (C8): 4186.01 Hz - The highest note on a standard piano.

Practical Use: Synthesis

When you are building a synthesizer in a language like Max/MSP or Pure Data, you use this conversion to translate user keyboard input into a signal for the 'phasor' or 'osc' object. Accurate conversion is the difference between a tuned instrument and digital noise.

Practical Use: EQ

If you see a peak in your frequency analyzer at 440 Hz and you want to know what note is causing it, you can work the formula backward (Hz to MIDI):
d = 12 √ó log2(f / 440) + 69

Why MIDI 69?

The MIDI standard assigns A4 to number 69 because it allowed enough room for lower octaves below the piano range while keeping "Middle C" at the intuitive number 60.

Pitch Bend Warning

Remember that MIDI Note Numbers are discrete (integers). If you use a Pitch Bend wheel, your synth is actually calculating frequencies between the integers on this chart!
