The ADSR Envelope of the Perfect Alert

In sound design, every sound has an ADSR Envelope (Attack, Decay, Sustain, Release). For short notification sounds, the "Sustain" should be near zero. You want the "Attack" to be incredibly fast—within 5 milliseconds—to grab the listener's primitive attention. However, if the "Decay" is too fast, the brain doesn't have time to process the pitch, making the sound feel like a "click" or a "glitch."

Our engineered alerts use a Logarithmic Decay. This creates a natural "ringing" sensation (like a bell) that feels pleasant while remaining highly visible. We detail these waveforming techniques in our Technical Specs hub.

Why Distinct Tones Matter

Psychologically, we suffer from "Notification Fatigue." When every app uses the same default "Ding," our brain learns to tune it out. By assigning a distinct SMS alert tone to your most important apps or contacts, you create a "Pavlovian Response"—you know exactly who is messaging before you even touch your phone.

Optimizing for Small Speakers

Small phone speakers struggle with low-frequency transients. If an alert relies on a "Thud" or a "Bass Kick," it will sound like a muffled vibration. Our notification sounds for Android and iPhone are high-pass filtered at 300Hz. This removes the energy that would otherwise cause your speaker to hit its "Xmax" (physical limit) and distort, resulting in a much cleaner, louder-sounding alert.
