Understanding Piano Frequencies

In modern music, we use a system called Twelve-Tone Equal Temperament (12-TET). In this system, every octave is divided into twelve equal parts (semitones). Because human hearing is logarithmic, this "equal" division is actually a geometric progression. Every semitone is separated by a factor of the twelfth root of two (approximately 1.05946).

The standard reference point for tuning is A4 = 440 Hz (often called "Stuttgart Pitch" or "Concert Pitch"). Using this anchor, we can calculate the frequency of every other key on the keyboard using the formula:
f = 440 √ó 2^((n - 49) / 12)
where n is the key number (1 to 88).

How to Use This Chart in Music Production

Understanding which frequencies correspond to which notes is a superpower for mix engineers. Here are three practical ways to use this piano frequency chart in your workflow:

1. EQ Carving for Clarity

If you have a muddy mix, find the fundamental frequency of your bass or melody part. For example, if your bassline is centered around G2, you know the fundamental energy is at roughly 98 Hz. By clearing space around this frequency on other tracks (like the kick drum or piano low-end), you allow the bass to breathe without excessive volume.

2. Removing Harsh Resonances

Often, a recording will have a "whistling" or ringing sound at a specific note. By matching the perceived pitch of the ring to our chart, you can pinpoint the exact frequency to apply a narrow Q "notch" filter. If a vocal rings on a high C6, look at 1046 Hz first.

3. Sub-Bass Management

Sub-bass is physically difficult to hear but uses a massive amount of headroom. Many "heavy" electronic tracks peak between F0 (21 Hz) and F1 (43 Hz). If you are producing for clubs, you need to ensure your sub-bass notes stay within the range where human subwoofers can actually reproduce the sound, typically staying above 30 Hz.

Physics: Overtones and Harmonics

While the table above lists the "Fundamental Frequency," a real piano key produces more than one single sine wave. When you strike a key, you are hearing a complex waveform made of the fundamental plus a series of harmonics (overtones). These overtones occur at integer multiples of the fundamental (2x, 3x, 4x, and so on).

This is why a piano sounds different from a synthesizer playing the same note. The "timbre" of the instrument is defined by the relative strength of these higher frequencies. When EQing, you aren't just adjusting the note itself, you are adjusting the character of the overtones.

Quick Formula

Calculating Hz manually? Use this:

f = 440 √ó 2^((n - 49) / 12)

Where n is the key number on an 88-key piano.

Pro Tip: A432 vs A440

Some listeners prefer 432 Hz tuning, claiming it sounds more "natural." Mathematically, 440 Hz is the standard for international consistency, but modern DAWs allow you to shift the master tuning easily.
